Feynman tool — physics expert guide

How to use the zone detection output, what each heuristic means, and how to give feedback that the developer can act on

Version 0.1  ·  March 2026  ·  Reviewed by Dev the Dev

What you are trying to do

You have a passage of physics text that needs visual support — a place where a diagram, animation, or simulation would help a student understand something the text cannot carry on its own. The Feynman tool finds those places for you and tells you what kind of visual would help most.

Your job is to paste the text, read what the tool flags, decide whether you agree, and — when a video gets made — tell the developer whether the physics is right. You do not write code. You write physics.

How to use the tool — step by step

  1. Open the Feynman tool in your browser You will see a text area and an Analyze button. Nothing else is required.
  2. Paste a physics passage Paste the section of text you want analyzed — a paragraph, a page, or a full section. The tool works best on passages of 150–800 words. Very short passages may not contain enough content to flag. Very long passages produce too many zones to act on at once.
  3. Click Analyze and wait The tool sends your text to an AI system that reads it for visual gaps. This takes 5–15 seconds depending on passage length.
  4. Read the output — starting with the Summary The Summary tells you what kind of text this is and how many visual gaps were found. If the summary does not match your sense of the passage, note that — it is the first signal that the tool may have misread something.
  5. Check each flagged zone against your own judgment For each zone, ask: do I agree this passage needs a visual? Do I agree with the type of visual recommended? If not, that disagreement is valuable feedback. Write it down.
  6. Pass your review to the developer The developer uses your confirmed zones to generate Manim videos. They need your sign-off on which zones to prioritize and any corrections to the "what to show" description.

What the output sections mean

Summary

Two or three sentences describing the text type and its overall visual gap. Read this first. If it mischaracterizes the passage, the zone detections below may also be off.

Zone detection table

A line per flagged zone. Shows the quote, the heuristic code (TD / SG / QR / SC), and the recommended visual type. This is the tool's shortlist — the places it believes a visual is most urgently needed.

Figure details

The full description of each zone: what the visual should show, how an animation should unfold, what a student should be able to control in a simulation. This is where your physics expertise matters most — read each "what to show" description and ask whether it is accurate.

Priority ranking

Each zone ranked Critical, Important, or Supplementary. Use this as a starting point, not a final order. You may disagree — a zone the tool ranks Important may be the most confusing passage in your chapter.

The four heuristics — what each one means

The tool flags each zone with one of four codes. These are not categories of visual — they are reasons why a visual is needed. Understanding the difference helps you evaluate whether the tool has diagnosed the problem correctly.

TD
Temporal / Dynamic

Something changes over time and the text cannot show the sequence. The student needs to see a process unfold, not just its start and end state.

Example: an electron entering a magnetic field and curving into a circular orbit
SG
Spatial / Geometric

Shape, direction, orientation, or three-dimensional configuration cannot be reliably reconstructed from words alone. The student needs to see the geometry.

Example: the orientation of the electric field around a dipole
QR
Quantitative Relationship

Two or more variables have a functional relationship that a graph or chart would make instantly clear — and the text is asking the student to hold that relationship in their head.

Example: how the period of a pendulum scales with length
SC
Student Control Required

The concept can only be understood by varying a parameter and watching the outcome change. A static visual is insufficient — the student needs to interact.

Example: how changing damping coefficient changes oscillation behavior
Your judgment call
The tool assigns one heuristic per zone. You may disagree — a passage may need both TD and SG treatment, for example. If you think the heuristic is wrong, say so in your feedback. The heuristic determines the type of visual that gets built.

How to give feedback the developer can use

When a video is rendered you will be asked to watch it and respond. The developer needs physics feedback, not production feedback. They do not need to know if the colours are wrong or the animation is too fast. They need to know if the physics is wrong.

Situation Useful feedback Not useful feedback
The force vector is pointing the wrong way "The Lorentz force on a negative charge moving right in a field pointing out of the page should point downward. The animation shows it pointing up." "The arrow looks wrong."
The motion does not match the physics "The orbit radius should decrease as the field strength increases. The animation shows them as independent." "Something is off with the circle."
A label is missing "The magnetic field vector B needs a label. Without it, a student cannot distinguish it from the velocity vector." "Needs more labels."
The sequence is wrong "The animation should show the field first, then introduce the moving charge. Showing the charge first removes the causal logic." "The order feels off."
One rule for feedback
State what is wrong and what the correct physics is. The developer cannot fix a physics error they cannot describe in code. Vague feedback produces another wrong video.

When you disagree with the tool

The tool will sometimes flag zones you do not think need a visual, miss zones you think are critical, or recommend the wrong intervention type. This is expected in v0.1. Your disagreement is not a problem — it is data that improves the system.

When you disagree, note three things: the quote, what the tool said, and what you would say instead. That is all the developer needs to adjust the tool's behavior.

This is a v0.1 guide. The tool and the workflow it supports are in active development. Expect the process to change in v0.2 once the first test renders have been reviewed.
